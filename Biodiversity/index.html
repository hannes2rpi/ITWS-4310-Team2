Biodiversity during the Ordovician

There are major changes in biodiversity during the Ordovician Period. The first graph is called the Sepkoski Curve named after Jack Sepkoski, a University of Chicago paleontologists and professor who created the graph. He used thousands of data sets of fossil occurrences from around the world that represents the work of hundreds of paleontologists. From the data he counted the number of marine families present in each time interval and plotted this curve.



The bottom axis represent geologic time dating back from 540 million years ago to the present (0 million years ago). The geologic time scale is also plotted along the X-axis the Paleozoic is colored blue and includes the Ordovician Period which is represented by an ‘O’. The y-axis represents the number of marine families living. It ranges from 0 at the bottom to 700 at the top. The higher the curve goes the more marine families that were present and the high the biodiversity was at that time.



The curve is shown in three colors, dark blue represents the Cambrian Fauna which is dominated by trilobites and inarticulate brachiopods . The Paleozoic Fauna is a lighter blue and includes, rugose and tabulate corals, bryozoans, articulate brachiopods, crinoids, and nautiloid cephalopods . The Modern Fauna in yellow includes scleractinian corals, foraminifera, bivalves, gastropods, cephalopods (excluding the nautiloids), star fish, echinoids, and bony fish . Even though the faunas are named for periods and eras they occur through all the eras and periods, but their abundances change.


During the Cambrian Period the Cambrian fauna is dominate, during the remainder of the Paleozoic Era the Paleozoic Fauna is dominate. During the Mesozoic and Cenozoic the Modern Fauna makes up the largest part of the curve. During the Paleozoic Era, after the initial Cambrian Explosion, there is a big increase in biodiversity during the Ordovician with the number of marine families climbing to ~500. The level of biodiversity then stays mostly level until the end of the Paleozoic Era. There are two mass extinction events during the Paleozoic the End-Ordovician Extinction and the Late Devonian Extinction events represented by red arrows. During these two Paleozoic extinction events the number of marine families drops quickly, but in each case they rebound quickly and return to the previous levels.

The second graph is similar to the first, but it focuses in on the Ordovician Period and shows part of the Cambrian and Silurian periods. Instead of families it shows us the number of genera living. During the Cambrian you can see the total number of genera is around 500 and most of them are part of the Cambrian Fauna.


At the start of the Ordovician two things happen, the total number of genera increases sharply tripling to about 1500, while at the same time the dark blue curve representing the Cambrian fauna decreases, going from about 450 Cambrian Fauna genera to about 200 genera. This change is known as the Great Ordovician Biodiversification Event (GOBE). The GOBE is the most significant increase of marine biodiversity of the Phanerozoic. At the End-Ordovician a mass extinction event occurs and 85% of all Ordovician species went extinct. The red arrow and sharp drop of genera on the curve mark this event. This extinction is link to massive changes in the Ordovician climate and advance and retreat of large glaciers. At the start of the Silurian the number of genera is around 700, but it quickly climbs back up to over 1500, similar to levels during the Ordovician.
